MAHATMA GANDHI

  • 1915 : Returned to India and joined the Indian National Congress

Detailed Biography

Mahatma Gandhi, born on October 2, 1869, was a pivotal figure in the history of India and the world. His early life was marked by his education in law at the Inner Temple, London, where he developed a keen interest in nonviolent protest. In 1893, Gandhi moved to South Africa to practice law, where he faced rampant discrimination. It was here that he began his lifelong commitment to civil rights and equality, developing his philosophy of Satyagraha, or nonviolent resistance.

Returning to India in 1915, Gandhi became a leader in the Indian National Congress, advocating for swaraj or self-rule. His campaigns for non-cooperation and civil disobedience against British rule were instrumental in rallying millions of Indians to the cause of independence. Perhaps one of his most famous acts of protest was the Salt March of 1930, a direct action against the British salt monopoly.

Despite numerous imprisonments, Gandhi's influence continued to grow. His vision of an independent India was realized on August 15, 1947, though it was marred by the partition of India and Pakistan, which he deeply opposed. On January 30, 1948, Gandhi was assassinated by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist who opposed his views on nonviolence and inclusivity.

Impact and Legacy

Mahatma Gandhi's influence extended beyond India, inspiring movements for civil rights and freedom across the world. His philosophy of non-violence and civil disobedience influenced prominent figures such as Martin Luther King Jr., Nelson Mandela, and Cesar Chavez. Gandhi’s methods were adopted in various contexts to challenge oppression and promote social change.

" The best way to find yourself is to lose yourself in the service of others. " :- Mahatma Gandhi

Global Influence

Gandhi's commitment to non-violence and his ability to mobilize millions through peaceful protest have left an indelible mark on global history. His techniques and teachings are studied and revered by activists and leaders seeking justice and equality. The United Nations celebrates his birthday, October 2, as the International Day of Non-Violence, underscoring his universal impact.
